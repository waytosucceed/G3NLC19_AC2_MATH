Multiplication Tables

Multiplication is a way of adding the same number again and again. A multiplication table helps us find answers quickly.

The 2 time table:

Multiplying by 2 is just adding the number to itself

The 3 times table:

Let’s learn the 3 times table.
Each finger has 3 creases.

To find 3 × 3, raise 3 fingers and count creases (9).

Therefore, 3 x 3 = 9.

4 times table:

Let’s learn the 4 times table.
We can use a simple trick to find result of any number times 4:

Multiply by 2, then double the result.

Example: 5 × 4 = ?
First, 5 × 2 = 10
Then, 10 + 10 = 20

5 times table:

Let’s learn the 5 times table.
When you multiply by 5, the answers always end in 0 or 5!
If the number is even ( 2 , 4 , 6, 8, 10, 12) → The answer ends in 0
If the number is odd ( 3, 5 , 7, 9, 11) → The answer ends in 5.

Example:

  • 2 × 5 = 10 (ends in 0)
  • 3 × 5 = 15 (ends in 5)

Also, the 5 times table follows a skip-counting pattern:
5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50, 55, 60

Order of multiplication:

Let's show that 1 × 2 = 2 × 1

  1. What is 1 × 2 ?
    When we say 1 × 2, it means "1 group of 2."
    Imagine you have 1 plate with 2 muffins in it.

    Now, count how many muffins you have on the plate. You will count 2 muffins.
    So, 1 × 2 = 2.

  2. What is 2 × 1?
    When we say 2 × 1, it means "2 groups of 1."
    Imagine you have 2 plates, but each plate has only 1 muffin.

    Now, count how many muffins are there in total. You will count 2 muffins (1 muffin in each basket, and there are 2 baskets).
    So, 2 × 1 = 2

Both 1 × 2 and 2 × 1 give the same answer: 2.


This shows that the order of numbers doesn't matter in multiplication.

For example:

  • 2 × 3 = 6 (We add 2 three times: 2 + 2 + 2 = 6)
  • 3 × 2 = 6 (We add 3 two times: 3 + 3 = 6)

Both give the same result: 6

Here’s another example:

  • 4 × 5 = 20 (We add 4 five times: 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 = 20)
  • 5 × 4 = 20 (We add 5 four times: 5 + 5 + 5 + 5 = 20)

Both give the same result: 20
